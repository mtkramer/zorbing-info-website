Zorbing is a popular activity in many countries outside of the United States. Zorbing typically involves zorbonauts climbing inside a large beach ball, called a zorb, and traveling by running or walking the zorb around a course. For a little more excitement, riders are strapped in and the zorb is rolled down a hill. Zorbs can be used in all weather condidtions including in the snow and on the water.

Zorbing's Evil Twin

Zorbing should not be confused with water balling or water walking.

Water balling involves riding in an air-tight ball over water and therefore is much more dangerous than zorbing. In fact, the first person to develop a water ball for public production almost drown in freezing water while demonstrating the product. For safety, water balls are tethered, which itself can also be hazardous by getting snagged on passing boats or debris.

The United States Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) urges people to avoid this acitivity due to potential for drowning, suffocation, heat exposure, and collisions with sunken debris or boats. All of these factors can be accounted for and the dangers can be minimized with a corresponding minimization of the fun.

Zorb Construction vs Water Ball

Zorbs are most commonly created from a toroid of PVC and filled with air. They can be formed into doughnut shaped spheres, ovals, or cylinders depending on the desired activity. This structure protects zorbonauts against external collisions and softens internal impacts by creating a cusion of air between interior and exterior walls. Zorbs are dimpled where ribs attach the layers together for structural integrity and durability. The ribs can be anchoring straps to maximize flexability and zorbonaut comfort, struts to increase collision protection, or even hollow tubes to allow air or water to move freely through the zorb. Lastly, zorbonauts have the option to be safety belted to an interior wall before engaging in high speed rolling.

Water balls only have one air-tight layer to minimally allow the rider to float and breathe for about 4 hours of low activity. Both water balls and zorbs can have handle grips for entry and exit or loops for pre-launch orientation and transport.

Zorb Safety

Typically, zorbing is a very safe activity. However, zorbing can occasionally cause bruises, dizziness, and vomitting. Sometimes sprains and fractures can happen when running inside them and when the entrance opening is not avoided. One person was killed by high speed collisions with rocks while rolling down a ski slope in Russia. A teacher was killed and a student was critically injured when a safety net failed to stop their zorb.

Please zorb responsibly and never alone.
